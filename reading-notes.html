Reading Notes

Here I keep short notes on papers that are especially relevant to my work in computational astrophysics, galaxy formation, and numerical methods. The list starts with the most recent papers and moves downward toward older foundations that still shape the way I think about simulations, feedback, and Galactic structure.

April 2026

Too Big to Quench? I. Constraining ISM Stripping of Dwarf Satellites in Milky Way-like Halos

arXiv:2604.07613

This paper is useful because it reframes environmental quenching in dwarf satellites as a problem of gas retention under ram-pressure stripping, rather than as a generic shutdown mechanism with a single outcome. Using idealized wind-tunnel experiments for dwarfs moving through Milky Way-like halos, the authors show that the efficiency of stripping depends strongly on satellite mass. Below a transition scale, stripping can remove the ISM much more efficiently, whereas more massive systems retain a denser central gas reservoir and are therefore harder to quench through stripping alone.

Read together with GalactISM (2024), Tango for Three (2020), and Where Did All the Gas Go? (2018), this note fits well with a broader theme already present on the page: global outcomes depend not only on the surrounding environment, but also on which gas phase is being removed and on whether the galaxy is massive enough to hold on to a dense central ISM.

February 2026

What Suppresses Star Formation in Bulge-Dominated Early-Type Galaxies?

arXiv:2602.12324

This paper matters because it focuses on a question that is often answered too quickly in galaxy-formation discussions: whether low star-formation rates in bulge-dominated systems follow directly from morphology, or whether the bulge is only one part of a larger balance involving gas supply, stability, heating, and feedback. The note is interesting because it keeps the structural interpretation connected to the gas cycle instead of treating morphology as a complete explanation by itself.

Read after GalactISM (2024) and in the longer feedback context of Jahn et al. (2021), this note extends a recurring pattern on the page: matching a quenched state is less informative than understanding which physical cycle actually produced it.

December 2025

GASTRO library II: Exploring Chemical Bimodalities in Disk Galaxies with GSE-like Mergers and Massive Star-forming Clumps

arXiv:2512.01293

This follow-up is valuable because it shifts the emphasis from halo morphology to the chemical structure of galactic discs. The paper explores how disk bimodality can emerge in systems that experience GSE-like mergers and strong early clump-driven star formation, making the point that chemical sequences are tied to the broader assembly history rather than to a single simple trigger. That is especially relevant for anyone interested in how merger-driven heating and early enrichment can leave long-lived signatures in abundance space.

Read after Stellar Halos with GSE Analogs (2024) and GASTRO I (2022), this paper fits naturally with other notes here that warn against one-to-one interpretations of integrated observables. In this case, the warning applies to chemical structure: similar disc bimodalities can reflect a more complicated early history than a single clean origin story.

September 2024

Learning the Universe: GalactISM simulations of resolved star formation and galactic outflows across main sequence and quenched galactic environments

arXiv:2409.09114

This paper is important because it reframes the high-resolution galaxy simulation not merely as a science run, but as a benchmark environment for testing ISM physics at scale. That idea is especially attractive for anyone interested in subgrid development: before exporting a model into large cosmological production runs, one wants a controlled setting in which the phase structure, turbulence, cooling, and feedback loop can be inspected in detail.

Read after The Evolution and Distribution of Superbubbles (2024), The Limits of Supernova Feedback (2016), and Superbubble Feedback I (2015), this note connects directly to the existing emphasis of the page: reproducing a galaxy is not enough if the unresolved or marginally resolved ISM cycle is not physically interpretable.

August 2024

Cosmological Simulations of Stellar Halos with Gaia Sausage-Enceladus Analogues: Two Sausages, One Bun?

arXiv:2408.02723

This paper is interesting because it explicitly asks how exceptional the Milky Way may be in merger-history terms. By examining cosmological systems with more than one GSE-like contributor, the authors test whether halo structure and debris morphology remain simple to interpret once the assembly history becomes more crowded. The answer is useful precisely because it complicates the naive picture rather than just reproducing it.

Read together with GASTRO I (2022) and Tango for Three (2020), this note extends a line of thought already present on the page: halo observables are powerful, but they become most convincing when morphology, kinematics, and assembly history are interpreted together.

March 2024

Evolution and distribution of superbubbles in simulated Milky Way-like galaxies

arXiv:2403.12135

This paper is especially useful because it shifts the feedback discussion from injection recipes alone to the actual life cycle of superbubbles inside galaxies. Instead of asking only whether clustered supernovae regulate star formation, the paper asks where superbubbles form, how they grow, and how they populate the ISM. That makes it a strong bridge between local feedback physics and galaxy-scale structure.

Read after The Limits of Supernova Feedback (2016), Superbubble Feedback I (2015), and Keller et al. (2014), this note meshes very well with the existing feedback sequence on the page, because it treats hot bubbles as resolved or semi-resolved structures with their own spatial ecology, not only as a bookkeeping device.

July 2023

Chemical Cartography of the Sagittarius Stream with Gaia

arXiv:2307.08730

This paper is particularly relevant because it moves the Sagittarius discussion from pure structure into chemo-dynamical detail. By mapping chemical information across the stream with Gaia, the authors show that Sagittarius debris retains informative internal structure rather than behaving like a fully mixed stellar population. That is exactly the kind of result that makes Sagittarius so useful as a bridge between resolved stellar populations and dynamical reconstruction.

Read after Phase-Space Properties of Sagittarius Debris (2022), Tango for Three (2020), and Where Did All the Gas Go? (2018), this note adds the chemical layer that the earlier dynamical notes leave open. It also fits naturally with the way the page moves from numerical methods toward physically interpretable tracers.

December 2022

Phase-space Properties and Chemistry of the Sagittarius Stellar Stream Down to the Extremely Metal-poor Regime

arXiv:2212.08249

This paper is useful because it treats Sagittarius debris not as an isolated stream problem, but as part of a more crowded halo phase space. Extending the analysis down to the extremely metal-poor regime matters because it complicates simple tagging arguments: debris identification depends on how well one separates structures that were produced by different accretion events but now occupy partly related regions of integrals-of-motion space.

Read after Tango for Three (2020), Where Did All the Gas Go? (2018), and The Influence of Sagittarius and the LMC on the Milky Way (2017), this note strengthens the existing orbital and structural discussion by adding a more explicit phase-space view of how different accretion histories can become entangled.

April 2022

GASTRO library I: the simulated chemodynamical properties of several GSE-like stellar halos

arXiv:2204.12187

This paper is a strong reference for connecting merger history to present-day Galactic structure in a cosmological setting. Rather than treating Gaia Sausage/Enceladus as a single static fossil, the authors compare several GSE-like analogues and ask how broadly similar mergers can still generate non-identical chemodynamical outcomes. That makes the paper especially useful for anyone who wants to discuss analogues without pretending that there is only one acceptable realization of a GSE-like event.

Read after Tango for Three (2020), The EAGLE Simulations (2014), and Vogelsberger et al. (2013), this note sits nicely at the intersection of two themes already present here: cosmological galaxy-formation modelling and the interpretation of stellar-halo relics as constraints on assembly history.

October 2021

Real and counterfeit cores: how feedback expands halos and disrupts tracers of inner gravitational potential in dwarf galaxies

arXiv:2110.00142

This is one of the clearest papers in this reading list for separating agreement in broad galaxy-scale observables from fidelity to the actual baryonic cycle operating in the inner halo. Using idealized dwarf-galaxy simulations with the moving-mesh code AREPO, the authors show that the SMUGGLE model can generate dark-matter cores, whereas runs based on the older Springel & Hernquist (2003) effective model remain cuspy under the same initial conditions. The main value of the paper is that it does not reduce the comparison to a simple “better versus worse” statement; instead, it asks which physical ingredients are required for a bursty, time-variable baryonic cycle capable of modifying the central potential.

Read after Marinacci et al. (2019), Vogelsberger et al. (2013), and SH03, this paper makes a methodological point that is highly relevant for subgrid work: reproducing some integrated observables does not by itself guarantee that the unresolved ISM and feedback cycle are being represented with the physical detail required for questions such as halo-core formation.

August 2021

Simulating Cosmic Structure Formation with the GADGET-4 Code

arXiv:2010.03567

GADGET-4 is best read as the modern public synthesis of the long GADGET lineage. The paper is not only about performance or software engineering; it also consolidates developments in gravity, adaptivity, time integration, parallelization, and hydrodynamics that emerged over nearly two decades of community use. In that sense, it functions both as a code paper and as a historical summary of how numerical priorities changed as simulations became larger and physically more ambitious.

In dialogue with Hopkins (2012), GADGET-2 (2005), and GADGET (2001), the paper is especially interesting because it preserves continuity with the traditional SPH lineage while also absorbing lessons from later critiques of mixing and contact discontinuities. For anyone working with public simulation codes, GADGET-4 is therefore important both technically and conceptually.

September 2020

Tango for Three: Sagittarius, LMC, and the Milky Way

arXiv:2009.10726

This paper is compelling because it rejects the habit of modelling the Milky Way, Sagittarius, and the LMC in pairwise isolation. The main point is dynamical, but its implications are broader: once several massive perturbers act together, debris geometry, orbital backtracking, and even the interpretation of present-day asymmetries become less clean than in simple two-body narratives.

Read after Where Did All the Gas Go? (2018) and The Influence of Sagittarius and the LMC on the Milky Way (2017), this note deepens the dynamical thread already present on the page by replacing a cleaner satellite-by-satellite picture with a coupled system view.

May 2020

The Arepo Public Code Release

arXiv:1909.04667

This paper matters less because it introduces a new physical ingredient and more because it turns an influential numerical framework into a documented public platform. AREPO had already become central to methodological discussions after the original moving-mesh paper, but the public release changes the status of the method from a reference in the literature to a tool that can actually be adopted, tested, compared, and extended by a wider community.

Read after Marinacci et al. (2019), Vogelsberger et al. (2013), and AREPO (2009), this paper marks the maturation of the moving-mesh program from numerical proposal to public research infrastructure. It is also an important counterpoint to the public GADGET lineage represented by GADGET-2.

September 2019

Simulating the Interstellar Medium and Stellar Feedback on a Moving Mesh: Implementation and Isolated Galaxies

arXiv:1905.08806

This paper is especially useful because it separates two questions that are often mixed together in discussions of subgrid models. The first is whether a model reproduces broad integrated observables such as stellar-mass growth and the global star-formation history. The second is whether it generates an interstellar medium whose structure, phase distribution, and feedback cycle look physically plausible. Marinacci et al. show clearly that these two issues are related, but not equivalent.

Read after Vogelsberger et al. (2013), Hopkins (2012), Dalla Vecchia & Schaye (2012), AREPO (2009), and SH03, this paper becomes a particularly strong reference for the distinction between effective regulation and realistic ISM structure. A galaxy may evolve reasonably at the integrated level while still representing the unresolved gas cycle in a very different physical manner.

August 2018

H2 Chemistry in Galaxy Simulations: an Improved Supernova Feedback Model

arXiv:1808.10184

This paper is especially valuable because it does not judge feedback only by its ability to suppress the star-formation rate. Instead, it asks what the feedback prescription does to the thermo-chemical state of the dense ISM, especially to the molecular phase. That is the correct question when the goal is not only regulation, but also a physically interpretable connection between feedback, cooling, and H2 chemistry.

Read after Keller et al. (2014), Dalla Vecchia & Schaye (2012), and Stinson et al. (2006), this paper is a strong reminder that “good global regulation” is not sufficient by itself. A feedback model may produce acceptable integrated behaviour while distorting the cold molecular phase in ways that are hard to interpret physically.

February 2018

The Sagittarius dwarf galaxy: Where did all the gas go?

arXiv:1802.09533

This paper is especially relevant because it asks a simple but physically important question about Sagittarius: if the system was once more gas rich, where did that gas actually go? By focusing on gas stripping and its fate in the Galactic environment, the paper turns the Sagittarius problem from a purely stellar-dynamical story into one that also involves the circumgalactic and disc gas cycle.

Read after The Influence of Sagittarius and the LMC on the Milky Way (2017), this note adds the baryonic side of a problem that is often discussed only in terms of orbits and stellar debris. It also pairs naturally with the ISM-focused notes elsewhere on the page by reminding us that gas history is often central to interpreting the final system.

October 2017

The Influence of Sagittarius and the Large Magellanic Cloud on the Milky Way Galaxy

arXiv:1710.02538

This paper is a useful reminder that the present-day Milky Way cannot be interpreted as a purely self-evolved disc. By modelling the joint perturbations induced by Sagittarius and the LMC, the authors show that warps, asymmetries, and phase-space structure may carry the imprint of multiple satellites acting on different timescales.

Read after Athanassoula et al. (2011) and GADGET-2 (2005), this note sits slightly apart from the subgrid sequence but still fits the page well, because it shows how much of Galactic structure depends on the live dynamical setting in which later chemo-dynamical interpretation has to operate.

Gasoline2: A Modern Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics Code

arXiv:1707.03824

Gasoline2 is interesting because it shows what an SPH code looks like after the major methodological critiques of the previous decade have been taken seriously. The paper incorporates improved kernels, diffusion terms, timestep handling, and force accuracy in a way that makes it an important representative of the post-classic SPH generation.

Read after Hopkins (2012), GADGET-2 (2005), and GADGET (2001), this note complements the solver history already on the page by showing that the response to SPH criticisms was not only the rise of moving-mesh methods, but also a substantial redesign within the SPH family itself.

Cosmological Galaxy Evolution with Superbubble Feedback II: The Limits of Supernovae

arXiv:1606.09206

This paper is valuable because it is much less triumphalist than many feedback papers. Rather than presenting one prescription as universally sufficient, it asks how far supernova feedback alone can realistically go in regulating a Milky Way-like system. That framing matters because it turns the discussion from model tuning toward the physical scope and limits of the mechanism itself.

Read after Superbubble Feedback I (2015), Keller et al. (2014), and Dalla Vecchia & Schaye (2012), this note preserves the critical tone that makes the feedback section of the page strong: the goal is not only to regulate star formation, but to understand what each subgrid channel can and cannot plausibly do.

May 2015

Cosmological Galaxy Evolution with Superbubble Feedback I: Realistic Galaxies with Moderate Feedback

arXiv:1505.06268

This paper is attractive because it asks whether a cosmological simulation can produce more realistic galaxies without relying on arbitrarily extreme feedback. The main point is not that feedback should simply be stronger, but that the hot phase generated by clustered supernovae needs to retain enough physical meaning to regulate the system efficiently at the galaxy scale. In that sense, the paper is a bridge between the superbubble idea and its cosmological application.

Read after Keller et al. (2014), The EAGLE Simulations (2014), and Dalla Vecchia & Schaye (2012), this note fits very well with the existing feedback sequence because it keeps returning to the same question: can one obtain realistic galaxies without giving up physical meaning in the hot ISM phase?

Evolution of galaxy stellar masses and star formation rates in the EAGLE simulations

arXiv:1410.3485

EAGLE is a major reference point because it presents a full galaxy-formation framework that is explicit about calibration, large-scale observables, and the practical role of subgrid choices in cosmological simulations. The paper is especially helpful when read alongside other model-building notes here because it is not trying to resolve the full ISM directly; instead, it asks how far one can go with a carefully constructed effective framework tied to observational constraints.

Read after Vogelsberger et al. (2013), Dalla Vecchia & Schaye (2012), and SH03, this note provides a useful counterpoint to the more ISM-explicit papers elsewhere on the page: it shows how effective models can still be extremely successful when the scientific target is the galaxy population rather than the resolved multiphase cycle itself.

May 2014

A Superbubble Feedback Model for Galaxy Simulations

arXiv:1405.2625

Keller et al. push the feedback discussion beyond isolated supernova events and toward clustered stellar feedback, emphasizing that unresolved feedback regions should not be treated as single-phase parcels. The superbubble model is attractive because it tries to preserve the hot interior and the evaporation of colder material into that bubble, rather than relying on a purely artificial cooling switch.

Read after Dalla Vecchia & Schaye (2012), Stinson et al. (2006), and SH03, this paper is best understood as a phase-structure paper. Its main point is that the unresolved hot bubble should retain physical meaning, because that hot phase is central to how feedback vents out of the disc and regulates star formation.

December 2013

A Model for Cosmological Simulations of Galaxy Formation Physics

arXiv:1305.2913

Vogelsberger et al. present a galaxy-formation model built for cosmological simulations with a modern hydrodynamical solver, combining radiative cooling, enrichment, stellar feedback, black-hole growth, and AGN feedback in a unified framework. What makes the paper especially useful is that it does not present the model only as a technical recipe; it evaluates the resulting galaxy population against a broad suite of observables.

Read after AREPO (2009), GADGET-2 (2005), and SH03, this paper illustrates a broader shift in the field: from effective star-formation regulation alone toward larger, more modular galaxy-formation frameworks calibrated against several observables at once.

June 2012

A General Class of Lagrangian Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics Methods and Implications for Fluid Mixing Problems

arXiv:1206.5006

This paper is one of the key responses to the criticism that traditional SPH formulations have difficulty with fluid mixing, contact discontinuities, and some instability problems. Rather than abandoning SPH, Hopkins derives a broader Lagrangian framework that preserves conservation while improving the treatment of interfaces. It is therefore important not only for the results it produces, but also for the way it reconstructs the equations themselves.

Read after AREPO (2009) and GADGET-2 (2005), this paper is best seen as a methodological answer to the growing realization that some apparent “physical” differences between galaxy simulations could in fact originate in the hydrodynamical formulation itself. Together with the moving-mesh literature, it helped clarify that solver design is part of the physics interpretation problem, not something fully separable from it.

March 2012

Simulating Galactic Outflows with Thermal Supernova Feedback

arXiv:1203.5667

This is one of the cleanest papers in the feedback literature because it identifies the numerical problem with unusual precision. Thermal feedback often fails not because thermal energy is intrinsically unphysical, but because the injected energy is spread over too much mass. The resulting temperature jump is too small, the cooling time is too short, and the feedback energy is radiated away before it can do dynamical work.

Read after GADGET-2 (2005) and SH03, this paper remains a foundational reference for making thermal feedback numerically viable without turning cooling off by hand. Even when one prefers mechanical or multiphase approaches, it is still essential for understanding why naive thermal injection so often fails.

June 2011

Comparisons of Different Codes for Galactic N-body Simulations

Athanassoula et al. 2011, A&A, 531, A120

Although this paper is focused on gravity solvers and pure N-body applications rather than baryonic subgrid physics, it is very useful for placing widely used astrophysical codes in a broader numerical ecosystem. The comparison is valuable precisely because it asks not only whether different methods can run, but how they differ in memory use, timing, scaling, and dynamical accuracy for the same class of problems.

Read after GADGET-2 (2005) and GADGET (2001), this paper reinforces an important practical point: before comparing subgrid physics, one often has to ask whether the underlying code architecture is well suited to the scientific problem in the first place.

October 2009

E pur si muove: Galilean-invariant Cosmological Hydrodynamical Simulations on a Moving Mesh

arXiv:0901.4107

This paper is one of the major turning points in computational hydrodynamics for astrophysics. Springel proposes a moving-mesh finite-volume method based on a Voronoi tessellation and a Godunov scheme, with the explicit goal of combining the adaptivity of Lagrangian methods with the shock-capturing strengths of Eulerian approaches. The paper is motivated by limitations that had become increasingly visible in both traditional SPH and fixed-grid methods.

Read after GADGET-2 (2005) and SH03, this paper is important because it changes the solver itself, and therefore changes the context in which later discussions of galaxy formation and subgrid realism must be interpreted.

February 2006

Star Formation and Feedback in Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamic Simulations I: Isolated Galaxies

arXiv:astro-ph/0602350

This paper is a classic because it helped define the blastwave-feedback style that became highly influential in SPH galaxy simulations. The preferred model does not merely inject stronger feedback in an arbitrary sense; it temporarily suppresses cooling inside the unresolved remnant, motivated by Sedov–Taylor and snowplow evolution, so that the feedback remains dynamically important for long enough to alter the disc.

Read after GADGET-2 (2005) and SH03, this paper is best understood as a historically successful workaround for unresolved remnant evolution. Later work would question the physical cost of the imposed cooling shutoff, but this paper remains fundamental for understanding why that family of models became so widely used.

May 2005

The Cosmological Simulation Code GADGET-2

arXiv:astro-ph/0505010

GADGET-2 marks the consolidation of the GADGET family into one of the most widely used public codes in cosmological simulation work. The paper presents a massively parallel TreeSPH code with adaptive time-stepping, TreePM gravity, and an entropy-conserving SPH formulation. Beyond performance, what made the code so influential is that it provided a scalable public framework that could host a very broad range of physical modules.

Read after SH03 and GADGET (2001), this paper can be seen as the point where a promising numerical platform became the standard technical home for a large fraction of later galaxy-formation modeling.

June 2002

Cosmological SPH Simulations: A Hybrid Multi-phase Model for Star Formation

arXiv:astro-ph/0206393

Springel and Hernquist introduce one of the most influential effective models for the unresolved interstellar medium in cosmological simulations. Star-forming gas is treated as a hybrid multiphase medium in which cold clouds, hot ambient gas, cooling, supernova heating, evaporation, enrichment, and winds are coupled through a subgrid equilibrium framework. Its importance lies in providing a practical way to model star formation and feedback when the true small-scale ISM structure is far below the numerical resolution.

Built on the computational framework established by GADGET (2001), SH03 became foundational because it showed how an effective ISM model could deliver self-regulated star formation in large cosmological runs. It is exactly this success, however, that later made it such an important target of critique in notes concerned with burstiness, multiphase structure, and feedback realism.

May 2001

GADGET: A Code for Collisionless and Gasdynamical Cosmological Simulations

arXiv:astro-ph/0003162

This is the natural starting point of the sequence. Springel, Yoshida, and White present GADGET as a public Tree+SPH code designed for cosmological structure formation and interacting galaxies, combining collisionless N-body dynamics and gas hydrodynamics in a massively parallel framework. The emphasis on adaptive timesteps, dynamic range, and public release was historically important because it helped define what a modern community code in computational cosmology could look like.

In retrospect, this paper is valuable not only for its algorithms, but because it establishes the platform on top of which much of the later story unfolds. In that sense, GADGET (2001) is less a final answer than the opening chapter of a long numerical lineage in galaxy-formation simulations.
